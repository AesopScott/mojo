Role Handoffs

Role handoffs are built on durable Markdown channel files that commonly communicating roles read and write. Channels carry the ongoing conversation; handoff packets track bounded work that needs status, evidence, and closeout.

View process Back: operating cadence

Why handoffs exist

A role handoff is how one role communicates with another role when the exchange needs durable memory. It is not approval to publish, deploy, expand authority, or create an autonomous agent.

Sender role

The sending role names the context, request, evidence, authority boundary, and why another role is needed.

Receiving role

The receiving role accepts, asks one clarifying question, escalates, acts, or hands the work onward.

Shared channel

The channel file preserves ongoing conversation, decisions, active handoff packets, and the next expected response.

Channel files

Roles that commonly communicate should share a Markdown channel file. Each role reads the channel before acting and appends meaningful updates after decisions, blockers, handoffs, reviews, or closeouts.

3-minute check goal

Every active role and agent should have a standing goal to check its relevant function channels for work every 3 minutes while it is operating.

ItemRule
ReadRead the relevant function channel and linked active packets.
DetectLook for new requests, assigned work, blockers, review needs, decisions, escalations, and closeout tasks.
RespondIf work exists, acknowledge it, act inside authority, ask one blocker question, or route it to the correct function channel.
RecordAppend when work is found, status changes, evidence changes, a blocker appears, or a closeout happens. Routine no-work checks do not need noisy log entries.
GuardrailThe 3-minute goal does not activate autonomous polling, production actions, external communication, spending, or authority expansion without an approved runtime and explicit permission.

Handoff process

Use this flow whenever work needs to move from one role to another: build, review, approval, escalation, research, memory update, operations, or closeout.

  1. Check cadence: Every 3 minutes while operating, the role checks relevant function channels and linked packets for work.
  2. Read channel: The acting role reads its shared channel file before responding to shared work.
  3. Append message: The sending role appends context, request, evidence, authority boundary, and expected response.
  4. Create packet when needed: If the work is bounded, risky, multi-step, or reviewable, create a handoff packet and link it from the channel.
  5. Acknowledge: The receiving role appends acceptance, one clarifying question, redirect, escalation, or blocker.
  6. Respond: The receiving role acts inside its authority, reviews, decides, drafts, escalates, or hands the work onward.
  7. Record: The role appends changed artifacts, decisions, evidence, verification, and memory updates to the channel or packet.
  8. Close or continue: Close the packet when the receiving condition is met, or continue through another channel or packet.

Communication types

Architecture-to-build is only one handoff type. The same packet shape works for ordinary role communication.

Request

One role asks another role for action, review, research, drafting, implementation, or coordination.

Decision

A role sends options, recommendation, evidence, and the decision needed from the receiving role.

Escalation

A role raises a blocker, risk, authority issue, incident, or approval need to the correct owner.

Status

A role reports progress, changed artifacts, verification, open risks, and next owner.

Memory

A role proposes or records durable knowledge, RAG updates, source changes, and retrieval notes.

Closeout

A role confirms acceptance criteria, evidence, memory updates, and whether another handoff is needed.

Packet requirements

A handoff is ready only when the receiving role can tell what is being asked, why it matters, what authority applies, and how to respond.

Status model

Status belongs to the packet. Authority belongs to the role contract. The two should not be mixed.

Drafting

The sending role is still assembling context, request, boundaries, and evidence.

Ready for receiver

The packet has one receiving role, one next action, and enough context to respond.

Accepted

The receiving role has acknowledged ownership or redirected the packet to a better role.

In progress

The receiving role is acting, reviewing, deciding, escalating, or drafting the response.

Ready for review

The response exists and needs acceptance, approval, or a next-role handoff.

Complete

The response criteria, evidence, and memory updates are done.
