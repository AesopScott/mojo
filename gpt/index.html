Using AI In The Business For The First Time

A practical class for employees receiving access to OpenAI tools. The goal is simple: use AI for drafts, ideas, summaries, structure, and learning while keeping judgment and sensitive data protected.

Training goal

Safe first use, not AI mastery

By the end, employees should understand what AI is useful for, what they are not allowed to enter, how to verify output, and how to use AI safely for everyday work.

edit_note

Use AI for drafts

Emails, agendas, FAQs, procedures, checklists, summaries, explanations, and rough training material.

verified_user

Keep data protected

Do not enter confidential, regulated, sensitive, or restricted information unless the use case is explicitly approved.

fact_check

Review before use

Employees own final work product. Facts, tone, audience, privacy, policy fit, and approvals still need human review.

Welcome and why we are doing this

AI is being introduced as a work assistant, not a replacement for judgment. This is a learning rollout. Nobody needs to be an expert on day one.

What AI is good for

Drafting, summarizing approved text, rewriting, brainstorming, explaining concepts, organizing notes, and creating first pass documentation.

What AI is not good for

AI is not a final decision maker, verified source of truth, legal authority, HR authority, compliance authority, or replacement for required approvals.

Make the main governance rule memorable: if you would not send it to an external vendor without approval, do not put it into AI.

Prompt patterns

Context, task, audience, format, constraints

Good prompts tell AI what role the output should play. The employee still reviews the result before using it.

Draft

Draft a professional email to [audience] explaining [topic]. Keep it concise, clear, and friendly.

Build a safe prompt

Human review

AI output is draft material

AI can sound confident and still be wrong. The review step is not optional for external use, high impact work, or anything policy sensitive.

Check facts

Names, dates, calculations, policy references, source claims, and anything that will be acted upon.

Check fit

Tone, audience, reading level, company voice, completeness, and whether the output answers the real question.

Check gates

Privacy, approvals, HR involvement, legal involvement, compliance involvement, finance review, and security review.

Demo two: meeting agenda

Demo three: review checklist

Wrap up

Five rules to remember

This is the closing slide and employee quick reference.

  1. Use AI for drafts, ideas, summaries, structure, and learning.
  2. Do not enter sensitive information unless the use case is explicitly approved.
  3. Treat AI output as a draft, not truth.
  4. Review before using or sending anything.
  5. Ask when unsure.
Optional extension

Obsidian and basic skills

Use these only if the group needs extra material. Keep the same rule: only approved content goes into AI workflows.

Connecting Obsidian

AI can summarize, outline, tag, restructure, or draft from approved notes. Keep sensitive notes out unless the use case is approved, and review AI generated notes before treating them as organizational knowledge.

Writing basic skills with AI

A skill is a reusable instruction pattern. It should define purpose, trigger, inputs, steps, output, boundaries, and examples. It also needs testing with a safe example before real use.
