AI Governance for Developers

Governance Policy Pack

Policies are the operating rules behind GAIN. They tell developers what must be recorded, reviewed, tested, disclosed, monitored, and escalated before an AI system becomes part of real public service work.

Student outcome Know which policy to use first.

Start with acceptable use and use-case review, then add data, oversight, procurement, incident response, and transparency as the risk and audience grow.

Why policies matter

Governance is not just a checklist. It is a set of enforceable decisions.

A framework names the areas to think about. A policy tells people what they are allowed to do, what they must not do, who approves the work, and what evidence needs to exist. For developers, policies become tickets, gates, test evidence, data restrictions, vendor requirements, release criteria, monitoring, and incident workflows inside GAIN.

Priority order

Build the policy stack in the order teams will need it.

1

Set the floor

Acceptable Use Policy defines what staff can and cannot do with AI right now.

2

Capture the work

Use Case Review Policy makes every AI project visible in GAIN before pilot or launch.

3

Control data and decisions

Data Handling and Human Oversight policies prevent unsafe inputs and unmanaged outputs.

4

Govern external risk

Vendor Procurement Policy turns vendor claims into contract and evidence requirements.

5

Operate in public

Incident Response and Public Transparency policies keep accountability alive after release.

Ownership model

Policies need owners, not just authors.

Business ownerDefines purpose, affected users, service impact, and operational success.
Technical ownerDocuments data flows, model behavior, integrations, testing, logs, and monitoring.
Governance leadMaintains GAIN records, risk tiering, approvals, review cadence, and control status.
Privacy / security / legalReviews sensitive data, rights, records, security, compliance, and exceptions.
Procurement ownerEnsures vendor disclosures, contract terms, audit rights, and incident commitments.
Communications / public engagementTurns transparency requirements into clear public notice and feedback paths.

GAIN implementation map

Where the policies show up in the tool.

  1. Use-case record: acceptable use, purpose, owner, data category, risk tier.
  2. Review pathway: required reviewers, approval conditions, and re-review triggers.
  3. Controls: human oversight, procurement requirements, transparency, monitoring, and incident plan.
  4. Evidence: vendor factsheets, test results, public notices, approvals, logs, and incident records.
  5. Operations: review cadence, ownership changes, monitoring status, and policy exceptions.
