Gov AI Now: Practical AI Governance for Public Agencies

A hands-on class for moving from AI enthusiasm to accountable adoption. You will learn how to turn governance into concrete development controls.

Developer focus: intake, risk tiering, procurement questions, human oversight, monitoring, and incident response.

Start here

AI governance is how an agency decides what can ship.

Governance answers the questions behind responsible AI use: what may be used, by whom, for what purpose, with what data, under what controls, and how the system is monitored after launch.

As a developer, your job is not to memorize policy language. Your job is to make the policy visible in the product lifecycle: intake, architecture, evaluation, release review, logging, monitoring, rollback, and user support.

Student prompt Think of one AI feature you have built or used. Where would an agency record the owner, data, risk, approval, and monitoring plan?

Key contrast

Ungoverned AI

  • Scattered tools and shadow pilots
  • Unclear data handling
  • Vendor promises without evidence
  • No inventory, monitoring, or redress

Governed AI

  • Approved use cases and named owners
  • Risk review before procurement or launch
  • Human oversight matched to impact
  • Incident response and public transparency

What you will leave with

A starter governance operating model.

  1. Explain AI governance in government work.
  2. Separate AI use, AI governance, and AI compliance.
  3. Map a use case to low, moderate, or high impact.
  4. Identify controls developers can implement and prove.
  5. Use the GovAI Coalition model as a practical reference, not as legal advice.

Case study

Gov AI Now is our course frame. The GovAI Coalition is the practical public-sector model.

Public sources do not point to one clear public entity named exactly "Gov AI Now," so this class uses that name as the learning theme. The focal case is the GovAI Coalition because it has practical resources public agencies can study and adapt.

The Coalition began in 2023 after agencies faced shared questions about privacy, safety, and responsible AI use. San Jose helped lead early work by creating generative AI guidance and sharing it so other agencies could adapt instead of starting from a blank page.

Developer lens

Shared standards become product requirements.

Policy template turns into acceptable-use rules and release criteria. Use-case worksheet turns into intake fields and review routing. Vendor factsheet turns into procurement questions, audit rights, and data terms. Incident plan turns into escalation, logging, evidence preservation, and rollback.

Scenario: city permit assistant

A city wants to deploy an AI assistant to help residents understand permit requirements and draft application materials. Your group will map the use case, choose a risk tier, and identify the controls needed before launch.

Facilitator prompts

Push the group from idea to controls.

  • What resident harm would make this unacceptable?
  • Where should the assistant refuse, escalate, or cite source policy?
  • What evidence would a developer need before release?
  • What must the vendor prove before integration?
  • What would you monitor weekly after launch?

Starter templates

Use these cards when your team needs governance before it has a full program.

AI Use Case Intake Form

Use this when: a team wants to try, buy, build, or materially change an AI-enabled system.

Minimum fields: owner, purpose, user group, data involved, decision impact, vendor/model, launch date, risk guess.

AI Risk Review Checklist

Use this when: a use case touches residents, employees, benefits, enforcement, public safety, or sensitive data.

Minimum fields: impact tier, affected groups, failure modes, required controls, approver, review interval.

Vendor AI Factsheet Questions

Use this when: procurement needs more than a sales answer about how the system works and how risks are managed.

Minimum fields: training data, model updates, security posture, testing results, audit rights, subprocessors, data retention.

AI Incident Response Mini-Plan

Use this when: an AI system produces harmful, inaccurate, insecure, biased, or unauthorized outputs.

Minimum fields: trigger, triage owner, containment, public notice threshold, evidence to preserve, relaunch criteria.

Public Notice Template

Use this when: residents or employees should know an AI system is being used in a service workflow.

Minimum fields: plain-language purpose, human role, data used, limitations, contact, appeal or correction path.

Department AI Policy Starter

Use this when: a department needs baseline rules before teams independently adopt AI tools.

Minimum fields: allowed uses, prohibited uses, data rules, approval path, training requirement, monitoring cadence.

Closing message

The goal is not to slow AI down.

The goal is to make AI use legible, accountable, and resilient enough to scale.

First 30 days

  1. Publish a draft acceptable-use policy.
  2. Open an AI use-case inventory.
  3. Create a lightweight review path for new AI systems.
  4. Add vendor AI questions to procurement intake.
  5. Define incident reporting and escalation.
