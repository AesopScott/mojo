# Draft Public Transparency Policy for AI Use

For: [Agency / Department Name]
Version: 0.1 draft
Effective date: [date]
Policy owner: [role / office]

## 1. Purpose

This policy defines when and how the agency provides public notice, explanation, feedback options, and review paths for AI systems.

The purpose is to make AI use understandable, accountable, and open to public trust and correction.

## 2. Scope

This policy applies to AI systems that:

- Interact with the public.
- Support public service delivery.
- Affect rights, benefits, eligibility, enforcement, employment, licensing, permits, safety, or access to services.
- Use personal or sensitive data.
- Produce outputs residents, employees, or applicants may rely on.

## 3. Transparency Principles

Public transparency should be:

- Plain-language.
- Easy to find.
- Specific enough to be useful.
- Updated when AI use changes.
- Connected to feedback, correction, or appeal.
- Proportionate to risk.

## 4. When Notice Is Required

Public notice is required for:

- Public-facing AI assistants or chatbots.
- AI systems that materially support decisions affecting people.
- High-impact AI systems.
- AI systems using personal or sensitive data in public service workflows.
- AI systems where people may reasonably believe they are interacting with a person.

Public notice should be considered for moderate-impact systems.

## 5. Notice Content

Public notice should include:

- Name or description of the AI system.
- Department or program using it.
- Purpose.
- Where it is used.
- What the AI does.
- What the AI does not do.
- Data categories involved.
- Human review role.
- Known limitations.
- How to ask questions.
- How to request correction or human review.
- Last updated date.

## 6. AI Inventory

The agency should maintain a public or internal AI inventory, depending on risk and legal requirements.

Inventory fields may include:

- System name.
- Department.
- Purpose.
- Vendor or model.
- Risk tier.
- Public-facing status.
- Data categories.
- Human oversight.
- Contact.
- Last review date.

## 7. Feedback and Redress

For public-facing and consequential systems, the agency should provide a way to:

- Ask whether AI was used.
- Report incorrect or harmful output.
- Request correction.
- Request human review.
- Appeal or contest an outcome when applicable.

Feedback should be routed to the system owner and tracked in GAIN or the agency record.

## 8. High-Impact Systems

High-impact systems require enhanced transparency.

Enhanced transparency may include:

- Public notice before launch.
- Use-case summary.
- Human oversight explanation.
- Data-use explanation.
- Evaluation or testing summary when appropriate.
- Appeal or correction instructions.
- Public engagement plan when appropriate.

## 10. Updates

Public notices must be reviewed and updated when:

- Purpose changes.
- Vendor/model changes materially.
- Data categories change.
- Risk tier changes.
- Public-facing functionality changes.
- Incident or complaint reveals notice is incomplete.

## 11. Responsibilities

Business owner:

- Ensures notice is accurate and understandable.

Technical owner:

- Documents system behavior, data flow, and limitations.

Communications/public engagement:

- Reviews clarity and accessibility.

Legal/privacy/security:

- Reviews obligations and exceptions.
