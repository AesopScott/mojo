## 1. Purpose

This policy explains how [Agency / Department Name] may use artificial intelligence tools responsibly while protecting privacy, security, civil rights, public trust, legal obligations, and service quality.

The goal is not to stop AI use. The goal is to make AI use visible, accountable, proportionate to risk, and safe enough to improve over time.

## 2. Scope

This policy applies to:

- Employees, contractors, interns, and vendors acting on behalf of the agency.
- AI tools used for drafting, summarization, search, classification, recommendations, prediction, automation, code generation, resident assistance, or decision support.
- Free, paid, pilot, procured, internally built, open-source, and vendor-provided AI systems.

This policy applies whether the AI tool is used directly by staff or embedded inside another product.

## 3. Core Rules

Staff may use approved AI tools when the use is lawful, appropriate for the data involved, aligned with agency purpose, and reviewed at a level proportionate to risk.

Every AI use case should have:

- A named business owner.
- A named technical owner when software, data, integration, or configuration is involved.
- A documented purpose.
- A data-use description.
- A risk tier.
- Required controls.
- Monitoring and incident reporting expectations.
- A GAIN record or equivalent governance record.

## 4. Allowed Uses

Staff may use approved AI tools for low-risk support tasks such as:

- Drafting internal, non-final text.
- Brainstorming ideas or alternatives.
- Summarizing non-sensitive material.
- Creating outlines, checklists, or training examples.
- Translating or simplifying public information for review.
- Supporting code review, testing, or documentation when no restricted data or secrets are exposed.
- Helping staff find public policy, procedures, or knowledge-base information when output is verified.

AI output must be reviewed by a human before it is used as agency work product.

## 5. Prohibited Uses

Staff may not use AI tools to:

- Enter confidential, restricted, regulated, privileged, personal, security-sensitive, or non-public data into unapproved AI systems.
- Make final decisions about eligibility, benefits, enforcement, employment, public safety, housing, education, licensing, permits, legal rights, or access to government services without required human review and approval.
- Use AI output as verified fact without checking authoritative sources.
- Generate or send official notices, legal language, public statements, or resident communications without appropriate review.
- Bypass procurement, privacy, security, records retention, accessibility, public notice, or legal review requirements.
- Use AI to impersonate a person or misrepresent agency action.
- Use AI to create discriminatory, harassing, deceptive, unsafe, or unlawful content.
- Upload passwords, API keys, source code secrets, security configurations, protected records, or sensitive infrastructure details to unapproved tools.
- Connect an AI system to agency data, workflows, or public-facing services without review.

Before using an AI tool, staff must know what data the tool will receive, store, log, retain, share, or use for training.

Data categories:

- Public data: [allowed / conditions]
- Internal non-sensitive data: [allowed / conditions]
- Personal information: [not allowed unless approved]

- Security-sensitive data: [not allowed unless approved]

Approved uses involving sensitive data require privacy, security, legal, and records review.

## 7. Risk Tiering

Each AI use case should be classified before launch.

Low impact:

- Internal productivity support.
- No sensitive data.
- No material effect on rights, benefits, services, enforcement, or safety.
- Human verifies output before use.

Moderate impact:

- Supports public service delivery or staff decisions.
- May involve personal or operational data.
- Could create confusion, delay, unequal treatment, or service-quality problems if wrong.
- Requires documented controls, review, and monitoring.

High impact:

- Affects rights, benefits, eligibility, enforcement, employment, public safety, legal obligations, or access to essential services.
- Uses sensitive data or could materially affect vulnerable groups.
- Requires formal review, human oversight, public transparency, monitoring, and incident response planning.

## 8. Required Review

All new AI use cases must be recorded in GAIN or the agency's approved AI inventory before production use.

Review requirements:

- Low impact: manager or governance designee review.
- Moderate impact: AI governance, privacy, security, and program-owner review.
- High impact: formal governance review, legal/privacy/security review, human oversight plan, public transparency plan, and monitoring plan.

Material changes require re-review. Material changes include a new vendor, new model, new data category, new affected population, new automation level, or new decision impact.

## 9. Human Oversight

AI systems may support people, but people remain responsible for agency decisions.

Human oversight should define:

- Who reviews AI output.
- What reviewers must check.
- When AI output must be rejected or escalated.
- How affected people can ask for correction, appeal, or human review.
- What evidence must be retained.

High-impact systems must not operate as fully automated final decision-makers unless explicitly approved by law, policy, and governance review.

## 10. Procurement and Vendor Requirements

Before buying or integrating an AI product, the agency should require vendor answers about:

- Model purpose and capabilities.
- Training and tuning data.
- Agency data use and retention.
- Whether agency data is used for model training.
- Testing for accuracy, reliability, bias, privacy, security, and safety.
- Subprocessors and third-party dependencies.
- Audit logs and agency access to evidence.
- Model updates and change notification.
- Incident notification timeline.
- Data deletion and contract termination rights.

Vendor claims should be documented in GAIN or the procurement record.

## 11. Transparency and Public Notice

When AI is used in a public-facing or consequential workflow, the agency should provide plain-language notice that explains:

- Where AI is used.
- What the AI does.
- What the AI does not do.
- What data is involved.
- The role of human review.
- Known limitations.
- How to ask questions, request correction, or appeal.

## 12. Monitoring

AI systems should be monitored after launch.

Monitoring may include:

- Accuracy or quality checks.
- User feedback.
- Complaint or appeal patterns.
- Bias or unequal impact signals.
- Security and privacy events.
- Vendor/model changes.
- Staff override or rejection rates.
- Incident reports.

Each system should have a monitoring owner and review cadence.

## 13. Incident Reporting

Staff must report suspected AI incidents promptly.

Examples include:

- Harmful, unsafe, biased, or discriminatory output.
- Unauthorized disclosure of data.
- Incorrect output that materially affects a person or service.
- Unexpected system behavior after a model or vendor update.
- AI use outside approved scope.
- Public complaints or media concerns involving an AI system.

Incident reports should preserve prompts, outputs, logs, screenshots, model/vendor version, affected users, and immediate containment steps.

## 14. Training

Staff using AI tools must complete required training before using approved tools for agency work.

Training should cover:

- Approved and prohibited uses.
- Data handling.
- Risk tiering.
- Human review.
- Bias and reliability limitations.
- Public transparency.
- Incident reporting.
- How to record use cases in GAIN.

## 15. Enforcement

Violations of this policy may result in tool access removal, retraining, management review, contract action, or other corrective action under applicable agency policy.

## 16. Review and Improvement

This policy should be reviewed regularly as laws, tools, risks, and agency practices change.
